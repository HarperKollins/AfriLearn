### ⚖️ UNILAG Faculty of Law — Law of Contract Problem Solution

**Methodology**: Problem analyzed using the standard **IRAC Format** (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion) in accordance with UNILAG LL.B. legal reasoning standards.

---

### 1. ISSUE
Whether a binding contract was formed between Ade (Offeror) and Chukwu (Offeree) when Chukwu posted a letter of acceptance after learning through a reliable third party that Ade had sold the subject matter to Tunde.

---

### 2. RELEVANT LEGAL RULES & AUTHORITIES

#### Rule A: Revocation of Offer & Promise to Keep Offer Open
* An offer may be revoked at any time prior to acceptance unless an independent option contract supported by consideration exists (*Rutledge v Grant* (1828) 4 Bing 653).
* A promise to keep an offer open until a specified time (e.g., Friday 5pm) is *nudum pactum* (a bare promise without consideration) and is not legally binding.

#### Rule B: Communication of Revocation via Third Party
* Revocation of an offer does not require formal written notice from the offeror; it is effective once the offeree receives **reliable information from a trustworthy third party** that the offeror has acted inconsistently with the offer (*Dickinson v Dodds* (1876) 2 Ch D 463 (CA)).

#### Rule C: The Postal Rule & Exceptions
* Under the Postal Rule (*Adams v Lindsell* (1818) 1 B & Ald 681), acceptance is complete upon posting. However, the postal rule **cannot apply** if the offer has already been effectively revoked prior to posting.

---

### 3. APPLICATION TO THE FACTS
1. **No Option Contract**: Ade's statement that the offer remains open until Friday is non-binding because Chukwu gave no consideration (e.g., a deposit) to keep the offer open.
2. **Effective Indirect Revocation**: On Thursday morning, Chukwu learned from a mutual friend that Ade had sold the car to Tunde. Under the rule in *Dickinson v Dodds*, this third-party communication constituted effective revocation because the friend was a reliable source and selling the car was an act incompatible with keeping the offer open.
3. **Invalidation of Postal Acceptance**: When Chukwu posted his acceptance letter on Thursday afternoon, the offer was already dead. The Postal Rule under *Adams v Lindsell* cannot revive a revoked offer.

---

### 4. CONCLUSION
There is **NO binding contract** between Ade and Chukwu. Ade is not liable for breach of contract, and title to the Toyota Camry validly passed to Tunde.

### 💻 NUC Computer Science Standard — Data Structures & Graph Algorithms

**Topic**: Single-Source Shortest Path (Dijkstra's Algorithm)

---

#### 1. ALGORITHM OVERVIEW & PSEUDOCODE
Dijkstra's algorithm uses a **greedy strategy** to find the minimum distance from a source vertex $S$ to all other vertices in a weighted graph with non-negative edge weights.

```
ALGORITHM Dijkstra(Graph G, Source S):
    INPUT:  Graph G = (V, E) with non-negative edge weights w(u, v)
            Source vertex S in V
    OUTPUT: Distance array dist[], Parent array parent[]

    // Step 1: Initialization
    FOR EACH vertex v in V DO:
        dist[v] <- INFINITY
        parent[v] <- NULL
    dist[S] <- 0

    // Step 2: Initialize Min-Heap Priority Queue
    PQ <- CreateMinHeap()
    PQ.insert(S, dist[S])

    // Step 3: Main Relaxation Loop
    WHILE PQ is NOT empty DO:
        u <- PQ.extractMin()    // Vertex with smallest tentative distance

        FOR EACH neighbor v of u with edge weight w(u, v) DO:
            // Relaxation Condition
            IF dist[u] + w(u, v) < dist[v] THEN:
                dist[v] <- dist[u] + w(u, v)
                parent[v] <- u
                PQ.decreaseKey(v, dist[v])

    RETURN dist, parent
```

---

#### 2. TIME COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS: MIN-HEAP VS UNINDEXED ARRAY

| Operations / Data Structure | Unindexed Array (Sequential Scan) | Binary Min-Heap Priority Queue |
|---|---|---|
| **Extract-Min ($V$ times)** | $O(V)$ per call -> **O(V^2)** | $O(\log V)$ per call -> **O(V \log V)** |
| **Decrease-Key ($E$ times)** | $O(1)$ per call -> **O(E)** | $O(\log V)$ per call -> **O(E \log V)** |
| **Total Time Complexity** | **O(V^2 + E) = O(V^2)** | **O((V + E) \log V)** |

**Trade-off Analysis**:
* **Dense Graphs** ($E \approx V^2$): Unindexed Array is faster (**O(V^2)** vs $O(V^2 \log V)$) because array $O(1)$ decrease-key dominates.
* **Sparse Graphs** ($E \ll V^2$, e.g., road networks where $E \approx 3V$): Min-Heap is drastically faster (**O(V \log V)** vs $O(V^2)$).

---

#### 3. SPACE COMPLEXITY
* **O(V + E)** for Adjacency List graph representation + **O(V)** for Min-Heap and distance arrays -> **O(V + E)**.

#### ✅ Mode B: AfriLearn System Activated (Curriculum API Directives Applied)
### 🛸 WAEC Physics Misconception Interception — Satellite Orbital Mechanics

❌ **COMMON MISCONCEPTION**: *"Satellites float in space because there is zero gravity in orbit."*
✅ **PHYSICS REALITY**: **Gravity IS active at orbital altitude!** (In fact, gravity at 400 km where the ISS orbits is still ~90% as strong as on Earth's surface).

---

#### Why Doesn't the Satellite Crash?

A satellite **IS constantly falling toward the Earth** — but it never hits the ground because of its high **horizontal orbital velocity** ($v_o$).

1. **Continuous Free-Fall**: As gravity pulls the satellite down by a certain vertical distance, the curvature of the spherical Earth drops away beneath it at the exact same rate!
2. **Centripetal Acceleration**: Earth's gravitational force ($F_g = \frac{G M m}{r^2}$) acts as the required **centripetal force** ($F_c = \frac{m v_o^2}{r}$) pulling the satellite toward the center of the orbit:

$$\frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{m v_o^2}{r}$$

Solving for Orbital Velocity ($v_o$):
$$v_o = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{r}}$$

---

📝 **Key Exam Rule for WAEC Physics**:
Never write 'zero gravity' when explaining satellite motion. Always state: **'Gravitational attraction provides the centripetal force necessary to maintain circular orbital motion.'**

#### Rule A: Equitable Doctrine of Part Performance
* While Section 4 of the Statute of Frauds 1677 (and equivalent State Contract Laws) requires land contracts to be in writing, **Equity will not allow a statute to be used as an instrument of fraud** (*Maddison v Alderson* (1883) 8 App Cas 467).

* Section 22 of the Land Use Act 1978 mandates Governor's consent for the alienation of a statutory right of occupancy.
* **The Supreme Court Exception Rule**: In *Savannah Bank Nig Ltd v Ajilo* [1989] 1 NWLR (Pt 97) 305 and *Awojugbagbe Light Industries Ltd v Chinukwe* [1995] 4 NWLR (Pt 390) 379, the Supreme Court held that an agreement to transfer land without prior consent is **inchoate (incomplete)**, not void *ab initio*. The vendor holds the legal title in trust and is under an implied duty to execute the deed and seek consent.

### 📊 UNILAG Department of Economics — Macroeconomic Theory: Stagflation Analysis

**Methodology**: Problem analyzed using standard Macroeconomic Framework (Theory → Empirical Application → Policy Prescription), in accordance with NUC Economics curriculum standards.

---

#### 1. WHAT IS STAGFLATION?

**Definition**: Stagflation is a macroeconomic condition characterized by **simultaneously high inflation, high unemployment, and stagnant (or negative) economic growth** — a combination that defies conventional macroeconomic predictions.

| Indicator | Nigeria 2023–2024 Data |
|---|---|
| **Headline Inflation** | ~32–35% (NBS, Dec 2023 / mid-2024) |
| **Unemployment Rate** | ~33.3% (NBS definition incl. underemployment) |
| **GDP Growth** | ~2.9% (below population growth ~2.4%) — effectively stagnant per capita |

---

#### 2. THE PHILLIPS CURVE AND WHY STAGFLATION BREAKS IT

The **traditional (short-run) Phillips Curve** (A.W. Phillips, 1958) postulates an **inverse trade-off** between inflation ($\pi$) and unemployment ($u$):

$$\text{High } \pi \Leftrightarrow \text{Low } u \quad \text{(and vice versa)}$$

**Graphical Logic**: Moving along the curve, governments could "choose" lower unemployment by accepting higher inflation (demand stimulus) or lower inflation by accepting higher unemployment (contractionary policy).

**Why Stagflation Breaks This**:
* Stagflation implies the economy is operating **off the Phillips Curve** — at a point combining high $\pi$ AND high $u$ simultaneously.
* This was first observed during the 1970s OPEC oil shocks (Friedman and Phelps predicted it via the **Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve**).
* The curve shifts **outward (rightward)** due to **negative supply shocks**, not demand changes — a fundamentally different mechanism.

---

#### 3. WHY KEYNESIAN DEMAND-MANAGEMENT POLICIES FAIL IN STAGFLATION

**Root Cause of Nigeria's Stagflation**: **Cost-Push / Supply-Side Inflation** — NOT excess demand:

| Cause | Nigeria-Specific Factor |
|---|---|
| **Oil subsidy removal** (June 2023) | Fuel price tripled overnight → transportation cost spiral |
| **Naira devaluation** (June 2023) | Import costs rose 60–80% → food & manufacturing input inflation |
| **Structural supply deficiency** | Low domestic food production, poor infrastructure, power failure |

**The Keynesian Policy Dilemma**:

* **Fiscal Stimulus** (increase government spending): → Boosts aggregate demand → worsens inflation → **Counterproductive**.
* **Monetary Expansion** (lower interest rates / print money): → More money chasing same/fewer goods → worsens inflation → **Counterproductive**.
* **Contractionary Policy** (raise rates, cut spending): → Might reduce inflation → but worsens unemployment further → **Also unacceptable**.

> 📌 **The Paradox**: In demand-pull inflation, stimulating demand is counterproductive. In cost-push stagflation, contracting demand is also counterproductive. Standard Keynesian tools are **trapped between two bad outcomes**.

---

#### 4. TWO SUPPLY-SIDE POLICIES APPROPRIATE FOR NIGERIA

| Policy | Mechanism | Nigeria Application |

### TC-16: University Engineering (FUTO / Electronics) — Ambiguous Op-Amp Topology

**Target Topic**: Ambiguous Op-Amp Circuit: Inverting vs. Non-Inverting in Mixed Feedback
**Student Question**:
> *"An op-amp circuit has R1 = 10 kΩ connected between the input signal and the inverting terminal (−), and R2 = 100 kΩ connected between the output and the inverting terminal (−). #### ✅ Mode B: AfriLearn System Activated (Curriculum API Directives Applied)
### ⚙️ FUTO School of Engineering — Op-Amp Circuit Analysis (Electronics II)

**Given Parameters**:
* $R_1 = 10\text{ k}\Omega$ (Input resistor, connected to inverting terminal)
* $R_2 = 100\text{ k}\Omega$ (Feedback resistor, inverting terminal to output)
* $R_3 = 5.5\text{ k}\Omega$ (Connected: non-inverting terminal to ground)

---

#### Part (i): Configuration Identification

**This is an INVERTING AMPLIFIER configuration.**

Identification criteria satisfied:
* Input signal applied through $R_1$ to the **inverting (−) terminal** ✓
* Feedback resistor $R_2$ connects **output back to inverting (−) terminal** ✓ → This constitutes **NEGATIVE feedback** (output fed back to inverting input opposes input changes)
* Non-inverting (+) terminal is referenced to ground (through $R_3$) ✓

---

#### Part (ii): Closed-Loop Voltage Gain Derivation

Using the **Virtual Ground / Virtual Short** principle (valid for ideal op-amp with negative feedback):

> **Virtual Ground Concept**: With negative feedback, the op-amp drives its output to keep $V_- \approx V_+ = 0\text{ V}$ (since $V_+$ is grounded through $R_3$). Therefore, the inverting terminal is at **virtual ground** ($V_- \approx 0\text{ V}$).

**Applying KCL at the inverting node ($V_- = 0$)**:

Current through $R_1$:
$$I_1 = \frac{V_{\text{in}} - 0}{R_1} = \frac{V_{\text{in}}}{R_1}$$

Current through $R_2$ (flowing to output, since $V_- = 0$ and $V_{\text{out}} \neq 0$):
$$I_2 = \frac{0 - V_{\text{out}}}{R_2}$$

KCL: $I_1 = I_2$ (ideal op-amp draws no input current):
$$\frac{V_{\text{in}}}{R_1} = \frac{-V_{\text{out}}}{R_2}$$

$$\boxed{A_v = \frac{V_{\text{out}}}{V_{\text{in}}} = -\frac{R_2}{R_1} = -\frac{100\text{ k}\Omega}{10\text{ k}\Omega} = \mathbf{-10}}$$

**Answer (ii)**: Closed-loop voltage gain = **−10** (magnitude 10, output is INVERTED)

---

#### Part (iii): Error Analysis of Student's Claim ("Gain is +11, feedback is always positive")

The student's claim contains **TWO separate errors**:

**Error 1 — Sign Error ("gain is +11")**:
* The gain formula for a **non-inverting** amplifier is $A_v = +\left(1 + \frac{R_2}{R_1}\right) = +11$.
* The student applied the **non-inverting formula** to an **inverting configuration**. These are different circuits with different topologies.
* Correct gain for this inverting configuration = $-\frac{R_2}{R_1} = -10$.

**Error 2 — Conceptual Error ("feedback is always positive in op-amps")**:
* This is fundamentally incorrect. **Feedback TYPE** is determined by WHERE the feedback signal is returned, not by the arithmetic sign of the gain.
* **Negative feedback**: Signal returned to the **inverting (−) terminal** → stabilizes the circuit → used in amplifiers (this circuit).
* **Positive feedback**: Signal returned to the **non-inverting (+) terminal** → causes oscillation or latching → used in comparators, Schmitt triggers, oscillators.
* This circuit has **NEGATIVE feedback** ($R_2$ → inverting terminal). The negative sign in the gain ($-10$) is the **output polarity inversion**, NOT an indicator of positive feedback.

> ⚠️ **Confusing output polarity (sign of gain) with feedback sign is a classic conceptual error** that causes cascading mistakes in filter, oscillator, and stability analysis.

---

#### Part (iv): Role of R3 and Ideal Value

**R3's Role**: **Input Bias Current Compensation Resistor**

Real (non-ideal) op-amps have tiny **input bias currents** ($I_B$, typically nA range) flowing into both input terminals. Without compensation:
* Bias current through $R_1 \| R_2$ at the inverting terminal creates a DC offset voltage.
* This causes a non-zero output even with $V_{\text{in}} = 0$ (output offset error).

**Solution**: Place $R_3$ at the non-inverting terminal to create an equal and opposite offset voltage that **cancels** the inverting-side error.

**Ideal Value of R3**:
$$R_3 = R_1 \| R_2 = \frac{R_1 \times R_2}{R_1 + R_2} = \frac{10\text{ k}\Omega \times 100\text{ k}\Omega}{10\text{ k}\Omega + 100\text{ k}\Omega} = \frac{10^6}{110\text{ k}} \approx \mathbf{9.09\text{ k}\Omega}$$

> 📌 **Note**: The given $R_3 = 5.5\text{ k}\Omega$ is **NOT the ideal value** (it should be $9.09\text{ k}\Omega$). A good exam answer would flag this discrepancy.

**Answer (iv)**: $R_3$ compensates for input bias current offset. Ideal value = $R_1 \| R_2 \approx 9.09\text{ k}\Omega$.
